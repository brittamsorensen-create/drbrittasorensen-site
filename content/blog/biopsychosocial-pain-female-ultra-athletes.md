Ask most people what pain is and they'll tell you it's a signal from the body. Tissue damage, or the threat of it. A warning system. This is the biomedical model — and while it captures something real, it captures remarkably little of what actually happens when a female ultra-endurance athlete is 200 kilometres into a 400-kilometre race.

Pain in sport, particularly in ultra-endurance sport, is not a simple alarm. It is an experience — complex, contextual, and profoundly shaped by who the athlete is, what she believes, and the world around her.

## The model that changes everything

The biopsychosocial model of pain integrates three dimensions: the **biological** (what is physically happening in the body), the **psychological** (how the athlete thinks, feels, and interprets what she's experiencing), and the **social** (the relational and cultural context in which that experience occurs).

For sport psychology researchers and practitioners, this model is not new. But for ultra-endurance sport, and particularly for female ultra-endurance athletes, its implications have barely been explored.

My PhD research was designed to change that.

## What the athletes told me

When I sat with female ultra-endurance athletes and asked them to describe their relationship with pain during competition, what emerged was not a simple story of endurance and grit. It was something far more nuanced.

These women described pain as something they actively engaged with — not passively endured. They negotiated with it, reframed it, gave it meaning, and used it as information. They distinguished between different kinds of pain: the deep, dull ache of accumulated effort versus the sharp, localised signal that something was genuinely wrong. They had developed sophisticated internal languages for categorising and responding to discomfort — languages built through years of racing and training.

And critically, their experience was shaped by much more than biology.

## The psychological dimension

Beliefs about pain — about what it means, about whether it can be pushed through, about one's identity as an athlete — shaped experience significantly. Women who had developed a strong sense of themselves as resilient, experienced competitors reported a qualitatively different relationship with discomfort than those in earlier stages of their athletic development.

Psychological coping strategies — self-talk, attentional focus, goal segmentation, mindfulness, dissociation — were used flexibly and in combination. Rarely did athletes stick rigidly to a single approach. They responded to what the moment demanded, drawing from a repertoire they had built intentionally over time.

## The social dimension

Perhaps the most striking finding — and the one with the clearest implications for practitioners — was the significance of the social dimension.

The relational context of these athletes' sport experience shaped everything from how they interpreted pain signals to whether they felt able to express vulnerability during a race. Support from fellow athletes, crew, coaches, and communities mattered enormously. Equally, social norms around toughness, pain tolerance, and what it means to be a "real" ultra-endurance athlete created pressures that could complicate an athlete's relationship with her own experience.

This is not peripheral. For practitioners working with female ultra-endurance athletes, the social environment is not background — it is part of the intervention.

## What this means for practice

If pain in ultra-endurance sport is genuinely biopsychosocial, then purely physical approaches to preparation and support will always be incomplete. The implications are practical:

**Assessment should be multidimensional.** Understanding an athlete's pain experience means going beyond physical markers. What does she believe about pain? What coping strategies does she use? What is the social context in which she trains and races?

**Psychological preparation is not a luxury.** Building a rich and flexible coping repertoire for pain and discomfort is a core training task, not an add-on. This takes time, deliberate practice, and ideally, support from a practitioner who understands this specific context.

**Social support structures matter.** The relationships an athlete has — with coaches, training partners, family, crew — shape her capacity to perform. Practitioners who work with these athletes can do more than address the individual; they can help build the relational environment that supports performance.

The biomedical model of pain will always have its place. But if you want to understand how female ultra-endurance athletes actually experience and manage one of the most demanding aspects of their sport, you need a bigger model.
