In 2024, halfway through a 100-mile ultra race (the Autumn 100), a female runner Leah calls her husband whilst running alone on the trail path, tears in her voice: "Feeling a bit low so I thought I'd phone you to cheer me up." A month later, circling a 400-metre track for 24 hours, another female runner Judith (24-hour track GB qualifier) is battling a cold, constipation, and a cutting headwind at the far end of the straight, trying desperately to gain a place at the World Championships. Both women were experiencing pain, and both finished their events. Leah finished the Autumn 100 to complete a Grand Slam series despite months of grieving for her mother's recent illness and subsequent death and undertrained legs. Judith made the qualifying distance for her GB selection. A combination of determination to achieve their individual goals, volitional high pain tolerance, and drawing upon their previous experiences helped them during two totally different ultra-endurance events.

Two studies from my PhD research at St Mary's University, London, provide unique insights into these lived experiences of ultra-endurance pain. The first explored the retrospective and reflective pain experiences of 13 experienced female ultra-endurance athletes across running, triathlon, open-water swimming, cycling, and adventure racing. The second captured pain experiences in real time, using think-aloud protocols during two running events. This blog brings together both these studies, comparing similarities and differences, and focusing specifically on what we can take away to use in practice.

## Pain is never just physical

Across both my studies, every ultra-athlete confirmed what pain scientists have defined: that pain is a multidimensional experience influenced by biopsychosocial factors — biology (physical sensation, fatigue, sleep deprivation, injury history, hormonal state), psychology (emotions, mood, confidence, previous pain memories), and social (support networks, cultural expectations, family responsibilities). These three domains interweave to influence the personalised pain experiences and coping strategies during training and racing.

## Pain perceptions: what are the differences between them?

Being able to distinguish between different types of pain and their sensory or affective qualities is an important process for developing coping strategies based on accurate pain assessment. Pain intensity is defined as the degree of discomfort that individuals experience when exposed to painful stimulation, whereas a person's pain tolerance level reflects the maximum intensity of a pain-producing stimulus that a runner is willing to accept in each situation — and is a very subjective experience. Pain intensity is primarily perceptual in nature, while pain tolerance is more behavioural.

Given that pain intensity is an important determinant of running, individuals who are better able to minimise their pain experience may be able to persist longer in pain-inducing running events. Pain tolerance could be influenced by mood, sleep deprivation, social pressure, trauma experiences, and life experiences. For example, one runner explained:

> "My personal background shaped my tolerance levels…I was brought up in an abusive home…so I was in the care system…I had such an isolated background, let's say it made me a lot stronger as I wasn't sugar coated."

Coming out of an abusive marriage, another athlete recounted: "I have actually been to worse places than that several times…when my life was literally falling apart…those times were a lot worse than any race will put me in." Speaking about her relationship with pain generally, she said: "Pain itself — it's just a mild inconvenience most of the time."

One of the most consistent findings across both my studies was that through reflecting on their pain experiences, the runners developed a tacit internal understanding of their pain. Through accumulated racing experience, they learn to distinguish between exercise-induced muscle pain, exercise-associated muscle cramp, and injury-related pain.

## What experience teaches us

In the retrospective study, some vivid examples of runners' perceptions illustrated the reflective learning from experience after an event or training that informs future pain coping techniques and planning. As one runner said:

> "I can sense when something is painful and hurting and is not normal, versus when it's painful and hurting and it's just the race process — the uncomfortableness of pushing yourself as hard as you can. Compared to a twinge, a pain that is going to lead to an injury."

Another drew a clear line between what she can run through and what she cannot: "It's across the boundary of breaking something or ripping something out — it's a whole physical trauma that takes over your body and your mind telling you to stop."

In the real-time study, both Leah and Judith explained how they had learned this distinction the hard way. Both had, in the past, ignored injury-level pains which later transpired to be serious stress fractures. For both, the decision to ignore their pain resulted in enforced extended time away from their running and transformed how they processed future pain experiences to prevent further injury.

## Pain coping strategies: a tried and tested toolkit for during the run

Our experiences of pain depend upon integrated sensory, affective, and cognitive inputs to our brains, which can be modulated and (sometimes) ignored using coping strategies that divert attention away from pain. Such strategies reported by the athletes in my studies included:

**1. Active distraction strategies**

Listening to music, podcasts, or audiobooks; talking to fellow runners; checking out the scenery and wildlife; or using disruption techniques (pulling funny faces, making noises, moving arms) to shift attention away from the pain: "It's very hard to think about discomfort and pain when you're smiling."

For Judith, music was a central coping tool, and as the hours passed she shifted from social interaction with other runners to music-based dissociation. "To block out that pain is a good thing," she said. Judith prepared different playlists for certain times she knew would be hard, selecting songs for uplifting her spirits, to sing along to, and keep her distracted from the anticipated running-related fatigue and pain.

**2. Temporal chunking**

Breaking the race into small, manageable goals ("Just get to the next aid station"); anchoring to loved ones for social support: "10 more miles and it's [husband]'s o'clock"; positive self-talk (e.g., "Come on legs!") and reframing to accept pain as a temporary sensation rather than a threat; walk–run pacing at deliberate intervals and distances to help tolerate the repetitive impact and muscle soreness.

**3. Nutrition management**

Due to previous experience of gastrointestinal issues during racing, both Leah and Judith carefully planned for their nutrition and hydration needs. Both were vegan so this was especially important as checkpoint food choices would not necessarily cater for their individual needs. Judith used humour to reframe her discomfort, most vividly when managing gastrointestinal issues (her "constapoo" running commentary to fellow athletes). This lightened the psychological weight of physical discomfort in a shared social environment.

**4. Social support**

The two events were very different when it came to crew support. Adhering to self-sufficient racing rules makes remote emotional support especially significant. The Autumn 100 was unsupported apart from at checkpoints — direct support at other times would have led to disqualification. However, during the third night-time leg, Leah called her husband for indirect emotional, safety, and pain support.

Her experience differed dramatically from the carefully organised 24-hour crew support set up by Judith like a military operation. Additional trackside support was provided for the elite athletes, who could also access a sports massage tent to soothe their pain or discomfort during the event. Judith reflected: "Back on it after massage, it's now 21.5 hours. 2.5 hours to go… make this misery end. So cold…I've had a massage, I've had a sleep, I've had nausea, tight legs, everything."

**5. Kit and physical adaptations**

Both athletes drew upon previous experiences to prepare for their expected pain and discomfort. Leah wore kinesio tape on an old posterior tibial tendon tear throughout the Autumn 100, having learned through reflecting on her pain experiences: "I've discovered that I can do a marathon or 50k without the tape, but it's gonna ache for days afterwards — whereas if I put the tape on, I don't." A variety of footwear options were also brought to manage the uneven, wet, and slippery terrain.

To conclude on the coping strategies, as one runner reflected:

> "I was always a person who wanted to do everything perfectly. When you run you must make mistakes and learn from them…experiencing a little bit of pain or discomfort is actually necessary if you want to reach a difficult goal…Ultra-running has taught me what would have taken decades of therapy…you must be in touch with your core values and who you are, how you react to pain."

## Exercise dependency: the wellbeing paradox

A theme that emerged from both my studies, which interdisciplinary practitioners need to understand, is the complex relationship between exercise, psychological wellbeing, and the risk of self-harm versus self-preservation when making sense of pain. For many of these athletes, training was not merely a hobby or competitive pursuit — it was a primary mechanism for managing mental health:

> "That first injury absolutely broke me, and obviously you can't break when you've got kids and your life to get on with…I just found everything really hard and I didn't learn straight away…now I'm more prepared to listen to my body and if it means I'm slightly more cautious than I ever was, I'd rather take a rest day, than continue beasting myself and then be out for months."

This creates a dangerous paradox. The psychological drive to maintain training, which supports an athlete's mental wellbeing, can also override the body's protective pain signals, leading to overuse injuries, delayed recovery, and ultimately longer time away from the sport.

## What this means for runners and practitioners

- **Develop your pain literacy deliberately.** The ability to distinguish the discomfort of effort from the pain of injury is a reflective skill. Medical practitioners and coaches can help athletes with pain education, encouraging them to practise internal body scanning during training.
- **Know your own dependency.** If your mental wellbeing depends heavily on training, that is worth acknowledging rather than treating it as simply motivational. Practitioners should create safe spaces for athletes to discuss this tension and help them develop alternative strategies for managing their mental health during enforced rest.
- **Build a pain coping toolkit — and know it's individual.** There is no universal coping strategy. Practitioners can help athletes identify and practise their specific strategies before race day and reflect on them afterwards.

## Conclusion

This article has highlighted the importance of paying attention to pain and offers holistic insight into how female ultra-endurance runners can interpret their sport-specific pain experiences. The findings illuminate how female athletes may manage distinctive physiological challenges, psychological processes, and social contexts. Fostering healthy environments is crucial to maintain a balanced psychological and physical wellbeing for the long term.
