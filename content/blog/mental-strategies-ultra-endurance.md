The sport psychology literature on coping strategies in endurance sport is substantial. The problem is that most of it was developed with shorter events, male athletes, or controlled laboratory settings in mind. When you are ten hours into a 24-hour cycling event, or walking into the fortieth kilometre of a 100km trail race, the strategies that looked elegant in a journal article can feel very far away.

My research involved talking in depth with women who had been to exactly that place — and come back. What they described, when pressed for the honest account, was a set of mental strategies that were practical, flexible, and often hard-won through experience.

Here are five of the most consistent.

## 1. Dissociation: looking outward when looking inward is too much

Dissociation — directing attention away from the body and towards external stimuli — has a mixed reputation in the sport psychology literature. Some argue that attending to bodily signals is essential for pacing and safety. They are not wrong. But the women in my research used dissociation strategically: not as avoidance, but as a tool for managing the point at which continued internal focus had become counterproductive.

Specific forms varied: focusing on the landscape, having conversations with fellow competitors, using music, engaging with the mechanics of the event. The key was intentionality — knowing when to look outward, and being able to return attention to internal signals when needed.

**Practical implication:** Develop a range of external focal points you can use deliberately. The goal is not to ignore the body, but to have somewhere else to put your attention when the body's signals are becoming noise rather than information.

## 2. Self-talk: the language you use matters

Every athlete uses self-talk. Not all of it helps. The research on this is relatively consistent: instructional self-talk (telling yourself what to do) tends to be effective for skill-based tasks; motivational self-talk (encouraging yourself, reminding yourself of your reasons) tends to be effective for endurance and effort-based tasks.

What emerged from my research was more nuanced. The women I spoke with had developed personal vocabularies — specific phrases, questions, and reframes that they knew worked for them, often discovered accidentally in races and then deliberately reinforced in training.

What those phrases had in common was that they were honest, specific, and grounded in the athlete's own experience and values. Generic motivational slogans rarely made the cut.

**Practical implication:** Develop your self-talk deliberately in training, not just in races. Pay attention to which phrases actually shift your state. Write them down. Test them when things get hard.

## 3. Goal segmentation: breaking the impossible into manageable

Long-distance racing involves a peculiar psychological torture: the knowledge of how far there is still to go. One of the most universally reported strategies among experienced ultra-endurance athletes is the deliberate reduction of the horizon — focusing on the next checkpoint, the next five kilometres, the next aid station, rather than the total remaining distance.

This is not denial. It is a deliberate allocation of attention to what is actionable. The athletes I spoke with were often very conscious of what they were doing — choosing to hold a smaller frame while holding the larger goal in reserve for when it was needed.

**Practical implication:** In training and race planning, practise shifting between different levels of goal focus. Know what your "next small target" system looks like before you need it under duress.

## 4. Reframing pain as information, not threat

One of the most significant differences I found between athletes with more and less developed coping repertoires was in how they interpreted pain. Less experienced athletes tended to experience intense discomfort as threatening — a signal that something was wrong, that they might need to stop. More experienced athletes had developed a more nuanced reading: distinguishing between the deep fatigue-based discomfort of sustained effort (which could be managed) and the sharp, localised, qualitatively different pain that signalled genuine injury risk.

This is a skill — and it develops through experience, reflection, and, ideally, informed support from practitioners who understand this distinction.

**Practical implication:** Spend time, ideally with a coach or sport psychologist, developing your own literacy for different kinds of pain signals. What does accumulated fatigue feel like in your body? How does it differ from injury-related pain? This isn't something you can read in a book — it requires deliberate attention over time.

## 5. Connection and social support as a performance resource

Perhaps the most underrated strategy on this list. Ultra-endurance events are often imagined as solitary pursuits — the athlete alone against the distance. The reality is more complex. The women in my research consistently described the significance of connection — with crew, with fellow competitors, with supporters, with the broader community of people who understood what they were attempting.

Social support is not just emotionally comforting. It is a performance resource. The presence of a crew member who knew when to say nothing and when to say exactly the right thing, a training partner who had been through similar experiences, a community that validated the endeavour — these were all factors that shaped how athletes experienced and managed their hardest moments.

**Practical implication:** Build your support environment with as much intentionality as you build your training plan. Who is in your crew? Do they know how you want to be supported? Have you ever actually told them?

---

None of these strategies are magic. None of them replace training, pacing, nutrition, or experience. But used deliberately and built over time, they represent a genuine competitive advantage — one that is still far less developed in most female ultra-endurance athletes than their physical preparation.
