## Where it began

My connection to sport and the body started long before the research. With a background in physical education, sport massage, and fitness instruction, I spent years helping people move better, recover faster, and understand their physical potential. I worked with athletes at all levels — from beginners finding their way into sport to elite performers preparing for competition — and through that work, a fascination grew.

What was it that allowed some athletes to push through extraordinary levels of discomfort? Why did some thrive in the most demanding conditions while others, equally fit, struggled to continue? The physical answers weren't enough. There was something deeper happening — something psychological.

## The research begins

That curiosity led me back to formal study. My MPhil opened the door; my PhD in applied sport psychology at the University of Central Lancashire built the framework. Over four years of doctoral research, I focused on a population that had been largely overlooked in the literature: female ultra-endurance athletes.

My research asked how these women experienced and managed pain during competition — not from a purely physiological perspective, but through a biopsychosocial lens that integrated the physical, psychological, and social dimensions of their experience. The findings were illuminating, challenging prevailing assumptions, and pointing towards a far more nuanced picture of what drives women to perform at the very edge of their limits.

## The athlete

I don't just study this from the outside. I am an active ultra cyclist and triathlete, competing in events that take me deep into the territory my research explores. This insider perspective is not incidental — it is central to how I work. I understand the loneliness of a long climb, the negotiation with pain at kilometre 200, the strange clarity that sometimes arrives when everything else falls away.

Sport and the outdoors have always been part of how I make sense of the world. Living in south-west France, with the Pyrenees on my doorstep, has only deepened that relationship.

## What drives me now

My PhD was a beginning, not an end. The landscape for female athletes in research, practice, and sport science remains frustratingly uneven. Women are still underrepresented in the studies that inform coaching practice, performance support, and clinical care in sport. That gap matters — and closing it matters to me.

This site is part of that work: translating research into accessible resources, connecting with athletes and practitioners who want evidence-based answers, and building a community of people committed to advancing the science and support of women in endurance sport.

I believe in doing this rigorously and accessibly — without oversimplification, without unnecessary jargon, and always with the athlete at the centre.
