## The Research Focus

My doctoral research sits at the intersection of applied sport psychology, pain science, and gender in sport. Specifically, I examined how female ultra-endurance athletes experience, interpret, and manage pain and discomfort during competition.

This is a field with surprisingly few rigorous answers. The majority of pain research in sport has focused on male athletes, or has treated sex and gender as incidental variables rather than central factors. My work was designed to address that gap directly.

## The Biopsychosocial Framework

Underpinning my research is the biopsychosocial model — the understanding that pain is not simply a physiological signal, but an experience shaped by psychological factors (beliefs, coping strategies, emotional state, identity) and social factors (culture, community, expectations, relational context).

For female ultra-endurance athletes, this framework opens up rich and important territory. How does a woman's relationship with her body, her history of sport socialisation, her identity as an athlete, and her social environment influence how she encounters and responds to extreme physical discomfort? These are not peripheral questions. They go to the heart of performance, wellbeing, and the kind of support that actually helps.

## The Three Studies

My PhD comprised three interconnected studies:

**Study 1** examined the lived experience of pain in female ultra-endurance athletes, using qualitative methods to surface the meaning-making processes that occur when the body pushes beyond ordinary limits. Participants described rich, nuanced relationships with pain — relationships shaped by years of training, specific racing contexts, and deeply personal psychological histories.

**Study 2** explored the coping strategies employed by these athletes, mapping the repertoire of psychological tools they draw on during competition: dissociation, attentional focus, self-talk, mindfulness-based strategies, social support, and more. Critically, it examined not just what strategies were used, but how they were deployed, adapted, and combined in real-race conditions.

**Study 3** brought the biopsychosocial model into closer contact with practice, examining how athletes' social and relational context — their support networks, their coaches, their communities — shaped both their experience of pain and their capacity to manage it effectively.

## Key Findings

Taken together, the research revealed:

- Female ultra-endurance athletes do not passively endure pain — they actively negotiate with it, drawing on sophisticated psychological strategies developed through experience
- The biopsychosocial model provides a significantly better explanatory framework for this population than purely physiological accounts
- Social and relational factors are underweighted in current practitioner approaches, despite playing a significant role in how athletes experience and manage discomfort
- There is a need for more gender-sensitive pain research in sport, and for sport psychology practice to better account for the specific context and experience of female ultra-endurance athletes

## Implications for Practice

These findings have direct implications for coaches, sport psychologists, physiotherapists, and other practitioners working with female ultra-endurance athletes:

- **Assessment** should go beyond physical markers to explore psychological and social dimensions of pain experience
- **Intervention** should be personalised to the athlete's existing coping repertoire rather than imposing a standardised approach
- **Support networks** matter — the relational context of an athlete's sport environment shapes her capacity to perform and recover
- **Practitioner knowledge** of this specific population needs to develop; generic sport psychology approaches may not serve these athletes well
